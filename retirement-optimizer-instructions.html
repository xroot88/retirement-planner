User Guide & Instructions

Overview

This tool projects your retirement finances year-by-year, optimizing withdrawals across multiple account types to minimize taxes while meeting your spending needs. It models Social Security, Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs), healthcare costs, and Roth conversions. The interface features a compact input form on the left and an interactive stacked area chart on the right showing your account balances over time.

Quick Start

  1. Enter your current account balances (left panel)
  2. Set your age, spouse's age (if married), and life expectancy
  3. Select your Social Security start ages (benefits auto-populate)
  4. Adjust your annual spending need
  5. Watch the chart update in real-time as you change inputs
  6. Review the year-by-year projection table below

The Balance Chart

The stacked area chart on the right side of the input panel shows how your account balances and Social Security income change over time:

  • Blue - Brokerage (taxable investment account)
  • Orange - Traditional 401k/IRA (pre-tax)
  • Green - Roth 401k/IRA (tax-free)
  • Purple - HSA (Health Savings Account)
  • Red - Annual Social Security income

The chart helps you visualize:

  • How your total portfolio grows or depletes over time
  • The shifting composition as you draw from different accounts
  • The effect of Roth conversions (401k shrinks, Roth grows)
  • When Social Security kicks in and how it grows with inflation
  • How SS income compares to your portfolio balances
  • When accounts become depleted

If you retire after age 55, you can withdraw from your 401k without the 10% early withdrawal penalty. Keep this checked if you qualify.

‚òëÔ∏è Aggressive Roth Conversion

When checked, the model reserves bracket space for Roth conversions instead of filling it entirely with 401k withdrawals. This shifts money from Traditional to Roth, paying taxes now at your target rate to avoid higher taxes later (especially on RMDs).

Effect: Higher conversions, faster 401k depletion, larger Roth balance over time.

Children on Health Insurance

Under the ACA, children can stay on a parent's health insurance until they turn 26. The model accounts for this:

  • Add up to 4 children with their current ages
  • Children are included in ACA premium calculations while eligible (under 26)
  • Child premiums are estimated at ~$300/month (vs ~$600 for adults)
  • The Federal Poverty Level (FPL) adjusts for household size, affecting subsidy eligibility
  • Supplemental healthcare costs also scale with children (30% per child)
  • Once a child turns 26, they're removed from your plan automatically
Tip: If you're retiring early with children still at home, their healthcare costs can significantly impact your expenses until they age out at 26.

Withdrawal Strategy Logic

The model follows this priority to minimize taxes while meeting spending needs:

  1. 401k withdrawals - Fill up to target tax bracket (minus room reserved for conversions)
  2. Brokerage withdrawals - Harvest gains in the 0% LTCG bracket
  3. Roth withdrawals - Tax-free, used to cover any shortfall
  4. HSA withdrawals - Tax-free for medical (or any purpose after 65)
  5. Additional brokerage - If still short, pull more (with LTCG tax)
  6. Additional 401k - Last resort, even if it pushes into higher bracket
Shortfall Alert: If the red "Funding Shortfall Detected" warning appears, your plan cannot meet spending needs in some years. Consider reducing spending, delaying retirement, or increasing savings.

Stress Testing Your Plan

Sequence of Returns Risk

A market crash early in retirement is more damaging than one later. To test this:

  1. Set Bad Ret % to a negative number (e.g., -10% or -20%)
  2. Set Start Year to when you want the crash to occur (0 = first year)
  3. Set Duration to 2-5 years
  4. Check if your plan still works (no shortfalls, reasonable final balance)

Test Different Crash Timings

Try these scenarios to understand your plan's resilience:

  • Early crash: Start Year = 0, Duration = 3 (worst case - depletes assets early)
  • Mid-retirement crash: Start Year = 10, Duration = 3
  • Late crash: Start Year = 20, Duration = 3 (usually less impactful)

Single Filer Scenario

To see what happens if one spouse passes away, switch to "Single" filing status. This shows the surviving spouse's situation with:

  • Single tax brackets (narrower, higher rates sooner)
  • Lower standard deduction
  • Only one person's Social Security

Tax Rules Modeled

  • Federal tax brackets - 2024 brackets, inflation-adjusted
  • Standard deduction - Including $1,550-$1,950 bonus per person 65+
  • Social Security taxation - Proper 0%/50%/85% brackets based on provisional income
  • Long-term capital gains - 0%/15%/20% brackets
  • NIIT - 3.8% on investment income above $200K (single) / $250K (married) - NOT inflation-indexed
  • Early withdrawal penalty - 10% before age 59¬Ω (waived with Rule of 55)
  • RMDs - Required Minimum Distributions using IRS Uniform Lifetime Table
  • IRMAA - Medicare premium surcharges for high-income retirees (with 2-year lookback)

IRMAA 2-Year Lookback

Medicare IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount) surcharges are based on your income from 2 years prior. The model now accounts for this:

  • A large Roth conversion in 2026 affects your Medicare premiums in 2028
  • The model tracks MAGI history and uses the appropriate year for IRMAA calculations
  • For the first 2 years of retirement, current-year MAGI is used as an estimate
  • Roth conversions are included in MAGI (they increase your income for IRMAA purposes)
Planning Tip: If you're 63-64 and planning large Roth conversions, be aware that IRMAA surcharges will hit when you turn 65 and enroll in Medicare.

Roth Conversion Sweet Spot Finder

Click the "üéØ Roth Sweet Spot" button to see an analysis of optimal Roth conversion amounts for Year 1 of your retirement.

What It Shows

  • Current Taxable Income - Your base taxable income before any additional conversions
  • Current Conversion - What the model is currently converting in Year 1
  • Recommended Sweet Spot - The optimal conversion amount with reasoning
  • Room to 12%/22% - How much you can convert before hitting each tax bracket
  • Room to IRMAA - How much before triggering Medicare surcharges (relevant if you're 63+)
  • Tax by Conversion chart - Visual showing how tax increases with conversion amount

How to Use the Sweet Spot Number

The sweet spot is a suggestion, not what the model is currently doing:

  • If Sweet Spot > Current: You could convert more. Consider enabling "Aggressive Roth Conversion" or manually planning larger conversions.
  • If Sweet Spot ‚âà Current: The model is already optimizing well for your situation.
  • If Sweet Spot = $0: You're already in a high tax bracket - not a good year to convert.

Why Convert to Roth?

The trade-off is: pay taxes NOW at your current rate to avoid taxes LATER when RMDs may push you into higher brackets. This is especially valuable if:

  • You're in a low-income year (before SS starts)
  • You expect tax rates to rise in the future
  • You want to leave tax-free money to heirs
  • You want to reduce future RMD amounts

HSA Withdrawal Rules

The model follows IRS rules for HSA withdrawals:

  • Any age: HSA can be used tax-free for qualified medical expenses (copays, dental, vision, prescriptions)
  • Before 65: Only used up to qualified medical costs to avoid 20% penalty
  • Age 65+: Can use for any purpose (medical = tax-free, non-medical = taxed like IRA, no penalty)
  • Medicare premiums: Base Medicare Parts A, B, D premiums are HSA-eligible after 65
  • NOT HSA-eligible: ACA premiums, Medigap/supplemental insurance, IRMAA surcharges

Using Guardrails in Monte Carlo

When "Use Guardrails" is checked, the simulation applies dynamic spending adjustments each year:

  • If withdrawal rate exceeds 6%, spending is reduced by 10%
  • If withdrawal rate drops below 4%, spending is increased by 10% (unless "Do Not Increase" is checked)
  • Adjustments accumulate over time

Guardrails Control Options

  • Do Not Increase: Spending only decreases in bad years, never increases in good years (more conservative)
  • Auto Calculate Spending: Uses automatic logic (can decrease to 50%, increase to 150% of original). When unchecked, you can set a custom "Max Decrease %" to limit how far spending can drop (e.g., 20% means spending won't go below 80% of original)

Guardrails Impact Statistics

When guardrails are enabled, these stats help you understand their effect:

  • Avg years reduced: Average number of years spending was cut across all simulations
  • Avg years increased: Average number of years spending was raised (will be 0 if "Do Not Increase" is checked)
  • Avg min spending: Average lowest spending level reached (as % of original). Example: 85% means on average, simulations bottomed out at 85% of planned spending.
  • Worst case: The absolute lowest spending level that occurred in ANY simulation (your "stress test" number). This represents the nightmare scenario - typically an early retirement bear market with slow recovery. Example: If worst case shows 65%, it means in the most severe 0.1% of outcomes, spending dropped to 65% of original. Ask yourself: "Could I survive on 65% of my planned spending?" If not, consider using manual mode with a smaller "Max Decrease %" (like 20%) or planning more conservative initial withdrawal rates.
Tip: Run the simulation both with and without guardrails. You'll typically see higher success rates with guardrails, but at the cost of potential spending cuts during bad markets. This tradeoff is the real value of the guardrails approach. The "Worst case" stat shows you the deepest cut you might face - use it to stress-test whether your plan has enough cushion.

Limitations & Disclaimers

Important: This tool is for educational and planning purposes only. It is not financial, tax, or legal advice. Consult a qualified financial advisor and tax professional before making retirement decisions.

The model does not account for:

  • Pension income or annuities
  • Rental income or other passive income
  • Part-time work in retirement
  • Tax loss harvesting
  • Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)
  • Estate planning / inheritance
  • State-specific tax rules (uses flat rate)
  • Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)
  • Roth 5-year rule for conversions
  • SS earnings test (if working before full retirement age)
  • Changes to tax law

Tips for Best Results

  • Use conservative assumptions (lower returns, higher inflation)
  • Plan to age 95+ to avoid outliving your money
  • Set Today Year to the current year and Retire Year to your target retirement date to see accumulation growth
  • Test the "bad sequence" scenario at different points (during accumulation and early retirement are worst)
  • Use the Roth Sweet Spot finder to optimize conversions
  • Compare aggressive vs. conservative Roth conversion strategies
  • If you're 63+, watch IRMAA thresholds when planning conversions
  • Run Monte Carlo with AND without guardrails to understand the tradeoff
  • A 70-80% Monte Carlo success rate is reasonable if you're flexible with spending
  • Review and update your plan annually
